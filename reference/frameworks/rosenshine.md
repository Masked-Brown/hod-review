# Rosenshine's Principles of Instruction

> **What this is.** The pedagogy anchor for the `PRINCIPLE` field of a finding, when the
> issue is about *how the lesson is sequenced and delivered* — how new material is
> introduced, practised, questioned, and reviewed. Summarised here in our own words;
> full sources at the foot. No AQA content lives in this file — the subject-specific
> anchor is `reference/spec/` (manifest M2.5). One file, one job.

## The claim in one paragraph

Barak Rosenshine took three bodies of evidence that rarely get read together — the
cognitive science of how people acquire and hold new information, direct observation of
what unusually effective teachers actually do in the room, and the research on cognitive
scaffolds — and found they converge on the same short list of teaching moves (Rosenshine,
2012). The list is not a theory of learning; it is a description of the instructional
behaviours that consistently precede learning. For an editor reading a lesson deck, it is
the most useful lens for a simple reason: most of its ten principles leave a visible trace
on a slide, so a deck can be read against them directly.

## The ten principles (in our words)

1. **Daily review.** Open by retrieving prior learning, not by presenting something new.
   The start of a lesson is the moment a class is most able to reconnect to what they
   already hold; spending it on review strengthens the ground the new material will stand
   on.
2. **Present new material in small steps.** Introduce content in short chunks and let
   students practise after each one, rather than delivering a long exposition and
   practising only at the end. Working memory overloads when too much arrives at once.
3. **Ask a large number of questions, and check every student's response.** Questioning is
   not a plenary ritual; it is how a teacher finds out whether the class is actually with
   them, for all students and not just the volunteers.
4. **Provide models.** Show worked examples and think-alouds that make expert reasoning
   visible, so students are not left to infer a method from the finished answer alone.
5. **Guide student practice.** Rehearse new material with the class before releasing them
   to work alone; spacing and prompting the first attempts protects them from practising
   an error into place.
6. **Check for understanding** *before* moving on — deliberately, not by asking "any
   questions?" and reading silence as mastery.
7. **Aim for a high success rate.** Rosenshine's read of the evidence is that effective
   teachers pitch guided practice so students succeed around 80% of the time: high enough
   that they are learning, not floundering; hard enough that they are not idling.
8. **Provide scaffolds for difficult tasks** — temporary supports (prompts, checklists,
   partial solutions) that are withdrawn as competence grows.
9. **Require and monitor independent practice** until recall and application become fluent
   and near-automatic, so that knowledge is available later without effortful searching.
10. **Weekly and monthly review.** Revisit material across weeks, not just within the
    lesson, so that what was learned is consolidated rather than forgotten.

Principles 1, 9, and 10 are where Rosenshine and the retrieval-and-spacing literature meet
(`reference/frameworks/retrieval-practice.md`); principles 2, 4, and 8 are where he meets
Cognitive Load Theory (`reference/frameworks/cognitive-load.md`). The three frameworks are
not rivals — they describe the same classroom from different distances.

## What it looks like when a slide gets it wrong

Kept deliberately generic — these are the shapes an editor learns to see, not worked
findings. Concrete, slide-anchored examples are generated from the constructed run and
live in `examples.md` (manifest M5).

- A **starter slide that opens on brand-new content** with no retrieval of last lesson —
  spends the highest-value minute of the lesson (principle 1).
- A single slide that **introduces several new ideas at once** with no practice step
  between them (principle 2).
- A slide that **states a rule or definition but shows no worked model** of it being used
  (principles 4 and 5).
- A lesson that **moves from exposition straight to independent work** with no guided
  practice and no check for understanding in between (principles 5 and 6).
- A deck that **never returns to earlier material** — no within-lesson or across-week
  review built in (principles 1, 9, 10).

Each of these is a *question for the teacher*, never a fix: the editor points at the
missing review or the absent model and asks how the teacher intends to handle it. It does
not write the starter.

## Citable handles (what a finding names under `PRINCIPLE`)

A finding cites one of these, not a vague gesture at "good practice":

| Handle | Flags |
|---|---|
| `Rosenshine 1 — Daily review` | No retrieval of prior learning at the start |
| `Rosenshine 2 — Small steps` | New material delivered in too large a block |
| `Rosenshine 3 — Question all students` | No checking of whole-class understanding |
| `Rosenshine 4 — Provide models` | A method asserted without a worked model |
| `Rosenshine 5 — Guided practice` | Exposition straight to independent work |
| `Rosenshine 6 — Check for understanding` | Progression assumed, not verified |
| `Rosenshine 7 — High success rate` | Task pitched to fail or to bore |
| `Rosenshine 8 — Scaffolds` | A hard task with no temporary support |
| `Rosenshine 9 — Independent practice` | No route to fluency/automaticity |
| `Rosenshine 10 — Weekly/monthly review` | No spaced return to the material |

## Boundaries — honest caveats

- Rosenshine describes **instructional behaviours that correlate with learning**, largely
  from process–product and master-teacher research; it is not a controlled account of
  mechanism the way Cognitive Load Theory is. Treat it as a checklist of moves, not a law.
- The principles are **strongest for teaching new, well-structured content** (definitions,
  procedures, models) and lighter guidance for open inquiry or extended project work. A
  deck of open discussion is not automatically "wrong" for having no small-steps sequence.
- The 80% success-rate figure is a **rule of thumb** Rosenshine drew from the literature,
  not a measured constant to hold a slide to. The editor uses it as a direction, not a
  threshold.
