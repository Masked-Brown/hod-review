# Retrieval Practice and Spacing

> **What this is.** The pedagogy anchor for the `PRINCIPLE` field of a finding, when the
> issue is about *how the lesson builds durable memory* — whether students are made to
> recall, whether practice is spaced and interleaved, or whether the deck only ever
> re-presents. In our own words; sources at the foot. No AQA content here (that is

## The claim in one paragraph

The single most robust finding in the science of durable learning is that the act of
*retrieving* something from memory strengthens it more than re-reading or re-explaining the
same thing (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006; Karpicke & Roediger, 2008). Memory is built by being
used, not by being exposed to. Two design consequences follow. First, a lesson should make
students *pull* knowledge back out — through questions, quizzes, brain-dumps, closed-book
tasks — not just push it back in. Second, that retrieval should be *spaced* across time and
*interleaved* across topics rather than massed in one block, because the very effort of
recalling something after a gap is what consolidates it (Cepeda et al., 2006). A large
review of learning techniques put practice testing and distributed practice at the top of
the list for classroom utility (Dunlosky et al., 2013).

## The mechanisms (in our words)

- **The testing effect (retrieval practice).** Being tested is not just measurement; it is
  learning. Retrieving a fact reconsolidates and strengthens the path to it, so the next
  recall is easier. Crucially this beats restudying even though restudying *feels* more
  productive — the fluency of re-reading is mistaken for durability (Roediger & Karpicke,
  2006).
- **The spacing effect (distributed practice).** The same amount of practice produces more
  durable memory when spread across sessions with gaps than when crammed into one. The gap
  lets a little forgetting set in, and recovering from that forgetting is what does the work
  (Cepeda et al., 2006).
- **Interleaving.** Mixing problem types or topics within a practice set, rather than
  blocking one type together, improves later discrimination and transfer — students learn
  not just *how* to do each kind but *which* kind they are looking at (Rohrer & Taylor,
  2007).
- **Desirable difficulties.** Retrieval, spacing, and interleaving all share a signature:
  they make practice feel harder and slower in the moment while making the learning last.
  Robert Bjork's frame is that these are *desirable* difficulties — conditions that depress
  immediate performance but raise long-term retention (Bjork, 1994; Bjork & Bjork, 1992).
  The corollary the editor watches for: a lesson optimised to *feel* smooth is often
  optimised against durable learning.

## Where this meets the other frameworks

Rosenshine's principles 1 ("daily review") and 10 ("weekly and monthly review") are this
literature seen from the classroom (`reference/frameworks/rosenshine.md`). The tension with
Cognitive Load Theory is real and worth holding: retrieval and desirable difficulties
*raise* in-the-moment effort, while load theory *cuts* it — the resolution is that load
should be cut on the *extraneous* packaging so capacity is free for the *desirable*
difficulty of recall (`reference/frameworks/cognitive-load.md`).

## What it looks like when a slide gets it wrong

Generic shapes, not worked findings (those are in `examples.md`, M5):

- A **starter that re-presents last lesson's notes** instead of asking the class to recall
  them (a review slide that does not retrieve).
- A lesson that **teaches, then immediately assesses the same content** with no spaced
  return — massed, not distributed.
- A **revision deck that only re-reads** the material — highlighting and summarising — with
  no closed-book retrieval anywhere in it.
- A practice set that **blocks one question type together** where interleaving would build
  discrimination.
- A lesson whose design choices all serve *feeling fluent* (worked answers on screen, no
  recall demand) — smooth in the room, weak by the exam.

The editor points at the absent retrieval or the massed practice and asks how the teacher
plans to build recall. It never writes the quiz.

## Citable handles (what a finding names under `PRINCIPLE`)

| Handle | Flags |
|---|---|
| `Retrieval — testing effect` | Re-presentation where recall was needed |
| `Retrieval — review vs re-reading` | A "review" that does not make students retrieve |
| `Spacing — distributed practice` | Practice massed instead of spread across time |
| `Interleaving` | One type blocked where mixing would aid discrimination |
| `Desirable difficulties` | Design optimised for fluency over durability |

## Boundaries — honest caveats

- Retrieval practice works best **once material is understood**, not as a substitute for
  first teaching it. Quizzing on something never taught is not retrieval; it is guessing.
- The **optimal spacing gap depends on how long the knowledge must last** — there is no
  single correct interval, so a finding flags *massed vs spaced*, not a specific schedule.
- These effects are **strongest for well-defined factual and procedural knowledge** and
  more variable for complex, ill-structured tasks. The editor does not treat every open
  task as a failed quiz.
- Much of the core evidence is from **controlled studies**; the EEF's read of how
  cleanly it transfers to ordinary classrooms is deliberately more cautious — see
  `reference/frameworks/eef-guidance.md`.
