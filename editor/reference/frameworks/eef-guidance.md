# EEF Guidance

> **What this is.** The pedagogy anchor for the `PRINCIPLE` field of a finding when the
> issue is best judged against *applied UK classroom evidence* rather than a single lab
> mechanism — and the calibration layer that keeps the other three frameworks honest about
> how cleanly they transfer to a real room. In our own words; sources at the foot. No AQA
> content here (that is `reference/spec/`, M2.5). One file, one job.

---

## Why EEF is a different kind of anchor

Rosenshine, Cognitive Load Theory, and retrieval-and-spacing are *mechanisms* — accounts of
how learning works. The Education Endowment Foundation is a *broker of applied evidence*: a
UK charity that synthesises and trials interventions and reports what actually moves outcomes
in ordinary schools, at what cost, and with what strength of evidence behind it. So the
editor uses EEF for two jobs the mechanism frameworks cannot do:

1. **Applied weight.** When a slide's problem is not a single mechanism but a broad
   design choice (feedback, metacognition, the purpose of a practical, disciplinary
   literacy), EEF's syntheses are the citable evidence that it matters in real classrooms.
2. **Calibration.** EEF's read of the cognitive-science literature is deliberately more
   cautious than the primary studies — it flags where a robust lab effect has *promising but
   variable* classroom evidence. That caution is a feature: it stops a finding from
   overclaiming.

## What the editor draws on

- **The Teaching and Learning Toolkit.** EEF's running meta-summary of approaches, each
  reported as an estimated *months of additional progress*, a cost, and a strength-of-
  evidence rating (Education Endowment Foundation, Teaching and Learning Toolkit). Feedback
  and metacognition/self-regulation consistently sit among the higher-impact, lower-cost
  strands. The exact month figures are **periodically revised as the evidence base grows**,
  so the editor cites the Toolkit for the *direction and relative weight* of an approach, not
  a frozen number.
- **Improving Secondary Science** (EEF guidance report). The most directly relevant document
  for this domain. Its recommendations are the applied backbone of a science read: surface
  and address students' **preconceptions and misconceptions**; **model** scientific
  reasoning and make thinking visible; build **memory and structured retrieval** of key
  knowledge; use **practical work with a clear learning purpose** rather than activity for
  its own sake; teach the **language and literacy of science** explicitly; and use
  **feedback** and self-regulation to move learning on.
- **Cognitive science approaches in the classroom: a review of the evidence** (EEF, 2021).
  The calibration source. It assesses spacing, retrieval practice, interleaving, managing
  cognitive load, and worked examples, and concludes the underlying science is strong but the
  *classroom* evidence for how, when, and for whom to apply each is more mixed — so they are
  promising principles to apply thoughtfully, not guaranteed recipes.
- **Metacognition and Self-Regulated Learning** (EEF guidance report, 2018). The basis for
  reading whether a lesson helps students plan, monitor, and evaluate their own thinking, or
  only delivers content.

## What it looks like when a slide gets it wrong

Generic shapes, not worked findings (those are in `examples.md`, M5):

- A slide that **states the correct idea but never surfaces the common misconception** it
  competes with — so the class keeps the wrong model alongside the right one (*Improving
  Secondary Science*: preconceptions).
- A **practical activity with no stated learning purpose** — students do the procedure but
  the science it is meant to teach is left implicit (*Improving Secondary Science*: purposeful
  practical work).
- A slide **dense with unglossed scientific vocabulary** treated as known (*Improving
  Secondary Science*: disciplinary literacy).
- A lesson that **delivers content but never asks students to monitor their own
  understanding** (Metacognition guidance).
- A **feedback step that only marks right/wrong** with no route to act on it (Toolkit:
  feedback).

The editor points at the missing misconception, purpose, or feedback loop and asks how the
teacher will handle it. It never supplies the wording.

## Citable handles (what a finding names under `PRINCIPLE`)

| Handle | Flags |
|---|---|
| `EEF Sci — preconceptions/misconceptions` | Right idea taught without confronting the wrong one |
| `EEF Sci — purposeful practical work` | Practical with no clear learning purpose |
| `EEF Sci — disciplinary literacy` | Scientific vocabulary/language not taught |
| `EEF Sci — modelling` | Scientific reasoning left implicit |
| `EEF — metacognition/self-regulation` | No support for students monitoring their thinking |
| `EEF — feedback (Toolkit)` | Feedback with no route to act on it |

## Boundaries — honest caveats

- EEF reports **average effects across trials**; an average that helps most classes can be
  wrong for a specific one. A finding cites EEF for *what the evidence supports*, then makes
  the case for *this* class from the intake — it does not substitute the average for the
  judgement.
- **Months-of-progress figures are estimates on a shifting evidence base** and are revised.
  Cite them for weight and direction, never as a precise, fixed measurement.
- EEF is a **UK** evidence broker aligned to English schools and the National Curriculum;
  that is exactly right for this AQA-scoped editor, and worth stating rather than assuming.
- Where EEF's cognitive-science review is more cautious than the primary retrieval/CLT
  studies, **the caution wins** — the editor does not use a lab effect to overclaim what the
  classroom evidence only partly supports.
